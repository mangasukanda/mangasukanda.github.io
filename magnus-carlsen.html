Who is Magnus Carlsen?

Magnus Carlsen was born on November 30, 1990, in Tnsberg, Norway, to chemical engineer Sigrun En and IT consultant Henrik Albert Carlsen. The family stayed in Espoo, Finland, for a year before returning to Norway in 1998, where they lived in Lommedalen, Baerum. They eventually relocated to Haslum. Carlsen showed an early affinity for academic difficulties. At two years old, he could do 500-piece jigsaw puzzles; at four, he enjoyed assembling Lego sets designed for youngsters aged 10 to 14.

His father, an avid amateur chess player, taught him to play when he was five years old, despite his initial lack of enthusiasm. He has three sisters and revealed in 2010 that one of the motivators for him to take up chess seriously was the desire to beat his oldest sister in the game.

Carlsen’s first chess book was Bent Larsen’s Find the Plan, and his first book on openings was Eduard Gufeld’s The Complete Dragon. Carlsen honed his early chess skills by moving the pieces around, seeking combinations, and recreating games and positions taught to him by his father.

Simen Agdestein underlines Carlsen’s extraordinary memory, noting that by the age of five, he could recollect the locations, populations, flags, and capitals of every country in the globe. Later, he memorized “virtually all” 356 Norwegian municipalities’ locations, inhabitants, coats of arms, and administrative centers. At the age of 8 years and 7 months, he competed in his first event, the youngest division of the 1999 Norwegian Chess Championship, and scored 6/11.

What does Magnus Carlsen do for a living?

Carlsen was coached at the Norwegian College of Elite Sport by Grandmaster (GM) Simen Agdestein, who credits Norwegian football manager Egil “Drillo” Olsen as a crucial inspiration for his coaching style. In 2000, Agdestein introduced Carlsen to Torbjrn Ringdal Hansen, a former Norwegian junior champion who went on to become an International Master (IM) and Grandmaster (GM), while Ringdal was doing a one-year siviltjeneste (alternative civilian service term) at the institution.

Carlsen’s rating increased from 904 in June 2000 to 1907 that year. In September 2000, he made his debut in the Norwegian junior team’s championship, scoring 312/5 against the country’s top junior players and earning a tournament performance rating (TPR) of about 2000. Apart from chess, which he studied three to four hours each day, his favorite interests included football and reading Donald Duck comic books. He also skied till he was ten years old.

Carlsen played nearly 300 rated tournament games, as well as various blitz tournaments and other lesser events, from the autumn of 2000 until the end of 2002. He finished sixth in the European Under-12 Championship in Pensacola in October 2002. The following month, he tied for first place in the 2002 World Under-12 Championship in Heraklion, finishing second on a tiebreak against Ian Nepomniachtchi.

He then earned three IM norms in a row, the first in January 2003 at the Gausdal Troll Masters (score 7/10, 2453 PR), the second in June 2003 at the Salongernas IM tournament in Stockholm (6/9, 2470 PR), and the third in July 2003 at the Politiken Cup in Copenhagen (8/11, 2503 PR). On August 20, 2003, he was officially awarded the IM title.
